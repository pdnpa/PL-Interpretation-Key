## Definition

{{ description }}

Lowland heath typically occurs in lowland landscapes where acidic soils, low nutrient availability and a history of grazing, burning or cutting have maintained dwarf shrub cover. It often occurs as a patchwork of heath with grassland, bare ground, wet hollows and developing scrub.

---

## Identification criteria

This class should be mapped when:

* Vegetation is dominated by dwarf shrubs (heather/heath-type structure)
* Habitat occurs in a lowland setting (generally below the moorland zone and not within open upland moorland landscapes)
* Heath forms patches or mosaics that are not simply small field-edge strips
* Scrub and trees are not the dominant canopy (if scrub dominates, map as scrub class; if trees dominate, map as woodland)

Lowland heath commonly occurs:

* On heathy commons, sandy or gravelly soils, and lowland plateaus
* As mosaics with acid grassland, bracken, wet heath and scrub
* Adjacent to tracks, rides and disturbed ground where bare patches may occur

---

## Aerial definition

From aerial imagery, lowland heath typically appears as:

* A mottled or textured vegetation surface compared with improved grassland
* Irregular patchwork of dwarf shrubs with interspersed grass/bare ground
* Boundaries that often follow soil and disturbance patterns rather than field edges
* Seasonal colour change, with heath often appearing darker / browner outside peak growing season

Where imagery is ambiguous, use landscape context (lowland vs upland, enclosure pattern, adjacent habitats) to support interpretation.

## In Protected Landscapes

Lowland heath is most likely in Protected Landscapes where lowland acidic substrates and historical heathland management persist (e.g., heathy commons, sandy/gravelly lowlands, coastal heaths where present). Extent is typically fragmented and often occurs as mosaics within wider semi-natural land.

---

### Management and drivers

Lowland heath structure and extent are influenced by:

* Grazing pressure and grazing type (ponies, cattle, sheep)
* Cutting, burning or rotational management
* Scrub encroachment in the absence of management
* Hydrological change affecting wet heath components
* Atmospheric nitrogen deposition and nutrient enrichment

Ecological condition should not be inferred directly from imagery alone.
